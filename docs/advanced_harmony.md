## Part 1: Famous Named Progressions

These are chord sequences that have become so widely recognized they have names. When a user references a sound or a song, check if a named progression is the right starting point.

### 1. The Pachelbel Canon Progression
**Formula**: I – V – vi – iii – IV – I – IV – V
**Key of D**: D – A – Bm – F#m – G – D – G – A
**Character**: Warm, cyclical, inevitable — feels like coming home over and over
**Famous uses**: "Memories" (Maroon 5), "Starships" (Nicki Minaj), "Go West" (Pet Shop Boys), "One" (U2 chord reference)
**Producer note**: Works in any key. The iii chord (F#m in D) is the secret — it keeps the progression from being too obvious.

### 2. The Axis of Awesome / Pop Progression
**Formula**: I – V – vi – IV
**Key of C**: C – G – Am – F
**Character**: Emotionally complete, universally satisfying — used in hundreds of hit songs
**Famous uses**: "Don't Stop Believin'" (Journey), "Let Her Go" (Passenger), "With or Without You" (U2), "Someone Like You" (Adele)
**Producer note**: Start on vi for a minor/darker version: Am – F – C – G — same chords, different gravity.

### 3. The vi–IV–I–V (Emotional Minor Start)
**Formula**: vi – IV – I – V
**Key of C**: Am – F – C – G
**Character**: More melancholic and emotionally driven than I–V–vi–IV despite being the same chords
**Famous uses**: Common in emotional pop, country ballads, teen pop
**Producer note**: The minor start creates a sense of longing. Use this when the I–V–vi–IV feels too resolved.

### 4. The Royal Road Progression (Japanese Pop)
**Formula**: IV – V – iii – vi
**Key of C**: F – G – Em – Am
**Character**: Bittersweet, nostalgic — extremely common in anime soundtracks and J-pop
**Famous uses**: Ubiquitous in Japanese pop music
**Producer note**: The iii chord is the magic here — creates a sense of reaching for something just out of grasp.

### 5. The 50s / Doo-Wop Progression
**Formula**: I – vi – IV – V
**Key of C**: C – Am – F – G
**Character**: Nostalgic, innocent, wholesome
**Famous uses**: "Blue Moon", "Stand By Me", "Earth Angel", "Johnny B. Goode"
**Producer note**: Apply lo-fi production and vinyl texture to make this feel fresh again.

### 6. The 12-Bar Blues
**Formula**: I – I – I – I – IV – IV – I – I – V – IV – I – V (turnaround)
**Key of A**: A7 – A7 – A7 – A7 – D7 – D7 – A7 – A7 – E7 – D7 – A7 – E7
**Character**: Cyclical, earthy, honest, emotional
**Famous uses**: Foundation of blues, rock, jazz, R&B
**Producer note**: Play all chords as dominant 7ths even on I — that's what makes it blues. Quick change variant: IV in bar 2 for more immediate harmonic movement.

### 7. The ii–V–I (Jazz Foundation)
**Formula**: ii7 – V7 – Imaj7
**Key of C**: Dm7 – G7 – Cmaj7
**Character**: Smooth, inevitable resolution — the most satisfying cadence in Western harmony
**Famous uses**: Every jazz standard. "Autumn Leaves", "What Is This Thing Called Love"
**Producer note**: In minor: Dm7♭5 – G7alt – Cm (half-diminished ii adds tension before the drop). Tritone sub: Dm7 – D♭7 – Cmaj7 (chromatic bass descent).

### 8. The Andalusian Cadence (Flamenco Descending)
**Formula**: i – VII – VI – V (descending)
**Key of A minor**: Am – G – F – E
**Character**: Dark, Spanish, fatalistic — descending motion feels inevitable
**Famous uses**: "Stairway to Heaven" intro, "Hit the Road Jack", "Sunny", countless flamenco forms
**Producer note**: The E major chord (V, major not minor) is the characteristic move — it's Phrygian dominant, creating the exotic Spanish tension.

### 9. The Sensitive Female Chord Progression
**Formula**: I – V – vi – IV (same as Axis) but with specific voicings
**Character**: Confessional singer-songwriter feel
**Famous uses**: Most acoustic-guitar pop of the 2000s–2010s
**Producer note**: The name is ironic/humorous but the progression is everywhere. Distinguished by its strummed acoustic guitar context.

### 10. The Creep Progression (Radiohead)
**Formula**: I – III – IV – iv
**Key of G**: G – B – C – Cm
**Character**: The borrowed iv (minor subdominant) creates that sudden, devastating dark shift
**Famous uses**: "Creep" (Radiohead), various emo/alternative songs
**Producer note**: The III (B major in G) is chromatic — it's borrowed from the parallel minor. The sudden shift to the minor IV after the major III is the emotional gut-punch.

### 11. The Circle of Fifths Progression
**Formula**: I – IV – vii° – III – vi – ii – V – I (full cycle)
**Character**: Maximum harmonic motion, each chord falling a fifth
**Famous uses**: Bach chorales, jazz standards, "Fly Me to the Moon"
**Producer note**: Use fragments of this (3–4 chords) as secondary dominant chains: E7 – A7 – D7 – G7 – C. Each chord is a secondary dominant of the next.

### 12. The Coltrane Changes
**Formula**: I – ♭III – ♭VI – I (major thirds cycle)
**Character**: Jazz-forward, surprising, each chord completely unexpected
**Famous uses**: "Giant Steps" (John Coltrane)
**Producer note**: Advanced — used to create maximum harmonic surprise within a short time span. Each chord center is a major third away.

## Part 2: When to Use Chord Inversions

An inversion is when a chord note other than the root is in the bass. Understanding when to reach for inversions is one of the fastest ways to elevate a progression from "functional" to "beautiful."

### Why Inversions Matter
- **Smooth voice leading**: An inversion can eliminate a large bass jump between chords
- **Bass line melody**: Inversions let you create a stepwise descending or ascending bassline while the harmony above stays relatively static
- **Color**: First and second inversions have a different emotional weight than root position — they feel less "settled"

### The Three Main Inversions

**Root Position (5–3 above bass)**: Bass = root. Sounds stable, grounded, conclusive.
- Use for: chord landings, strong beats, resolutions

**First Inversion (6–3 above bass, 3rd in bass)**: Bass = 3rd. Sounds gentle, floating, slightly unstable.
- Use for: passing between chords, connecting root positions, softer landings
- Notation: C/E means C major with E in the bass

**Second Inversion (6–4 above bass, 5th in bass)**: Bass = 5th. Sounds tense, yearning, unstable.
- Use for: before a strong resolution (cadential 6-4 before V–I), dramatic moments
- Notation: C/G means C major with G in the bass

### Practical Inversion Situations

**The Descending Bass Line (most musical use)**:
Want a bassline that walks down: C → B → A → G? Use:
- C (root) → G/B (G chord, first inversion) → Am → G
- The bass moves C → B → A → G stepwise while the harmony above changes
- This is exactly what Portishead does in "Glory Box"

**Softening a IV → I**:
Instead of F → C (big bass jump down a fourth):
Use F → C/E (C in first inversion) — bass moves F → E, just a half step

**The Cadential 6-4**:
Before a V – I resolution, use I in second inversion:
C/G – G – C
The bass stays on G for two chords before resolving — creates powerful forward pull

**Slash Chords vs. True Inversions**:
- G/B = G chord with B in bass = first inversion (B is the 3rd of G) — true inversion
- G/F = G chord with F in bass — F is not in G major, so this is a "slash chord" or pedal point, not a true inversion
- Both are useful; slash chords with non-chord bass notes create more exotic tension

### When NOT to Use Inversions
- Don't put the leading tone (7th scale degree) in the bass — creates awkward tension
- Don't use 2nd inversion at the start of a phrase — it sounds like the phrase hasn't started yet
- Sub bass in electronic music should almost always double the root — inversions in the upper register, root in the sub

### Inversions in Ableton
- To create an inversion in the Piano Roll: keep the chord notes the same, move the bass note down an octave
- Example for C/E (C major, first inversion): Notes are E2 (bass), then C4 – E4 – G4 above
- The sub bass track should still play the ROOT (C) — the inversion lives in the mid-register chord track

## Part 3: Secondary Dominants

A secondary dominant is a dominant 7th chord that tonicizes (temporarily creates a tonic feel around) a chord other than the actual tonic.

### The Core Concept
In C major, G7 → C is the primary V7 → I. But you can apply this logic to ANY chord in the key:
- D7 → G: D7 is V7/V (the dominant of the dominant)
- A7 → Dm: A7 is V7/ii
- E7 → Am: E7 is V7/vi
- B7 → Em: B7 is V7/iii

The secondary dominant adds a leading tone (raised 7th) pulling strongly toward the target chord.

### Why They Sound Good
The raised 3rd of the secondary dominant creates a chromatic leading tone — a half-step pull into the target chord's root. This is stronger than normal diatonic motion.

### Secondary Dominant Chains (Circle of Fifths)
Chain them together for cascading motion:
**E7 → A7 → D7 → G7 → C**
Each chord is a dominant pulling to the next. The bass falls by fifths. This feels inevitable and harmonically rich.

### Common Secondary Dominant Uses

**V7/V (D7 in C) — "Double dominant"**:
C – D7 – G – C
The D7 before G gives the G chord extra arrival weight. Common in jazz and gospel.

**V7/vi (E7 in C) — Tonicizing the relative minor**:
C – E7 – Am
E7 pulls toward Am like a temporary tonic. Creates drama before arriving on the vi chord.

**V7/IV (C7 in C) — Blues borrowing**:
C – C7 – F
Adding ♭7 to the I chord (C becomes C7) creates a pull toward IV (F). This is the blues move — it's why the I chord in blues is always a dominant 7th.

### Secondary Dominants in Electronic Music
- Use sparingly — one or two per progression, not every chord change
- They work especially well before an emotional landing chord (the vi or the IV)
- In minor keys: ♭VII7 → ♭VI is a common dark secondary dominant move

## Part 4: Dark, Sexy, and Crunchy Progressions

These progressions are curated for tracks that need weight, tension, and seduction. Organized by emotional character.

### 4.1 Dark and Unresolved (Suspended Tension)

**"The Massive Attack"** — Modal drone with bimodal flicker
- i – I (minor to major, same root): Cm → C
- Works because: never resolves, toggles between dark and light versions of the same note center
- Reference: "Angel" by Massive Attack
- BPM range: 70–110

**"The Phrygian Drop"** — Andalusian with electronic weight
- i – ♭VII – ♭VI – V: Am – G – F – E
- Works because: descending motion feels inevitable, E major (Phrygian V) adds exotic tension
- Reference: "Stairway to Heaven" intro, countless dark electronic tracks
- BPM range: 80–140

**"The NIN Loop"** — Industrial minimal
- Cm7 – F: two chords, repeated indefinitely
- Works because: extreme minimalism creates hypnotic atmosphere, all expression comes from production not harmony
- Reference: "Closer" by Nine Inch Nails
- BPM range: 100–120

**"The Tritone Menace"** — Maximum dissonance
- i – ♭V: Am – E♭
- Works because: the tritone (augmented 4th / diminished 5th) between A and E♭ is maximum dissonance, the "devil in music"
- Reference: Black Sabbath, NIN "March of the Pigs"
- BPM range: 100–140

### 4.2 Sexy and Cinematic (Dark but Inviting)

**"The Glory Box"** — Chromatic voice leading descent
- Dm – Dm/C – Bm7♭5 – BMaj7
- Works because: bass descends stepwise while a top note (B♭) stays constant — "someone walking away while you stay still"
- Reference: "Glory Box" by Portishead
- BPM range: 60–80
- Teaching note: The Bm7♭5 (half-diminished) is borrowed from Eb Dorian — modal interchange adding exotic color

**"The Bond Theme"** — Minor line cliché, jazz-influenced
- i – iM7 – i7 – i6: Am – Amaj7 – Am7 – Am6
- Works because: chromatic descent in the upper voice (A–G#–G–F#) over a static bass — the "James Bond" sound
- Reference: John Barry's 007 theme, countless spy/noir soundtracks
- BPM range: 80–110

**"The Two Feet"** — Heavy minor with space
- i – v – ♭VI – ♭VII: Am – Em – F – G
- Works because: minor v chord (Em, not E7) keeps it dark; ♭VI–♭VII adds forward motion without resolution
- BPM range: 85–100

**"The Angel Descend"** — Reznor-influenced chromatic
- Dm – A♭ – G – Fm
- Works because: Dm → A♭ is a tritone relationship (maximum tension), then resolves partially to G before Fm adds another dark color
- Reference: "March of the Pigs" (NIN)
- BPM range: 100–130

### 4.3 Crunchy and Energetic (Dark but Driving)

**"The Dorian Funk"** — Minor but bright, funky edge
- i – IV (Dorian): Am – D
- Works because: the major IV in a minor context is the Dorian signature — darker than major, brighter than pure minor
- Reference: "Oye Como Va" (Santana), countless funk and electronic tracks
- BPM range: 100–128

**"The Dark Euphoria"** — Minor with driving resolution
- i – ♭III – ♭VII – ♭VI: Am – C – G – F
- Works because: moves through major chords while staying anchored in minor — creates energy while maintaining darkness
- Reference: Common in melodic dubstep, melodic house drops
- BPM range: 128–140

**"The Deftones Shimmer"** — Power + texture
- ♭Bm – A♭ (power chord movement)
- Works because: half-step chord movement creates maximum dissonance between chords while power chords keep it grounded
- Reference: "Diamond Eyes" chord architecture
- BPM range: 100–120

**"The Supersonic FM Riff"** — Bass music over minor loop
- i – ♭VII – ♭VI – ♭VII (fast): Am – G – F – G
- Works because: ♭VII creates a Mixolydian flavor even in minor context — forward motion without resolution
- Reference: Common in bass house and dubstep builds
- BPM range: 130–145

### 4.4 Sexy with Jazz Color (Sophisticated Dark)

**"The Neo-Soul Descent"** — Extended voicings, unresolved
- im9 – ♭VIImaj7 – ♭VImaj7 – ♭IImaj7
- Example in A minor: Am9 – Gmaj7 – Fmaj7 – B♭maj7
- Works because: each chord is voiced as a maj7, creating a floating ambiguity; the B♭maj7 is borrowed from A Phrygian
- BPM range: 70–95

**"The Portishead Drift"** — Half-diminished tension
- i – iv – ♭II – i: Cm – Fm – D♭ – Cm
- Works because: ♭II (Neapolitan) chord creates maximum surprise — a major chord one half step above the tonic
- Reference: Portishead harmonic language
- BPM range: 60–85

## Part 5: The "1 Main + 3 Suggestions" Framework

When generating progressions, always lead with one primary recommendation, then offer three alternatives with distinct emotional directions.

**Format**:
- **Primary**: The progression that best matches the prompt
- **Darker**: A version with more tension or modal darkness
- **Brighter**: A version with more resolution or harmonic warmth
- **Unexpected**: A surprising option the user might not have considered — often the most interesting

**Example for "melancholic lo-fi minor key"**:
- Primary: Am – F – C – G (i–VI–III–VII — unresolved, looping)
- Darker: Am – F – Dm – E (i–VI–iv–V — more traditional minor cadence, heavier landing)
- Brighter: Am – G – F – G (i–VII–VI–VII — Dorian-ish, more forward motion)
- Unexpected: Am – Amaj7 – Am7 – Am6 (the Bond/line cliché — chromatic descent in one chord)
