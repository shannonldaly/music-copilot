# Automation Playbook
## Music Co-Pilot Knowledge Base
### Used by: Sound Engineering Agent, Production Agent, Teaching Agent

Automation is not a finishing touch — it IS the arrangement. The difference between a flat track and a track that breathes, builds, and releases is almost entirely automation. This document teaches WHEN to reach for automation, WHERE in the song arc to apply it, and WHAT specific settings to use.

---

## Part 1: The Core Principle

**Automation creates the illusion of a live performance.**

A static synth pad sounds like a preset. The same pad with a slowly opening filter, subtle volume swells, and a reverb that deepens in the breakdown sounds like something alive. Your ear perceives automation as intention — as if a human hand is guiding the sound in real time.

**The golden rule**: Automate what you want the listener to feel, not what you want them to notice.
- Obvious automation (a filter opening dramatically) is a technique
- Invisible automation (subtle volume riding to keep a vocal upfront) is mastery
- Both are valid — the question is always: what does THIS moment need?

## Part 2: Automation by Song Section

This is the most important section. Most producers ask "how do I automate?" The better question is "WHEN do I automate?" Here is a section-by-section guide.

### INTRO (bars 1–8 typically)
**Goal**: Orient the listener, establish the world, create forward pull without giving everything away.

**What to automate**:
- **Filter cutoff on pads/chords**: Start closed (low-pass at 200–400 Hz), open slowly over 4–8 bars toward full frequency. The listener doesn't hear a pad, they hear a pad arriving.
- **Reverb wet/dry on pads**: Start at 80–100% wet (distant, atmospheric), gradually dry out as the verse approaches (20–40%). This brings the music "toward" the listener.
- **Volume on individual elements**: Start lower, ride up gently. Don't start at full level — leave room for the track to grow.
- **High-pass filter**: Elements can start heavily high-passed (cutting below 500 Hz) and gradually open up, revealing bass frequencies as the track builds.

**Specific settings**:
- Filter start: 200–400 Hz (low-pass cutoff)
- Filter end (after 4 bars): 6–10 kHz (near fully open)
- Automation shape: Linear or slow logarithmic curve (not a step)
- Reverb wet in intro: 60–80%
- Reverb wet entering verse: 20–30%

**Ableton tip**: Use Draw Mode (B) and set grid to 1 bar. Draw a diagonal line from low value to high value. Right-click the line and choose "Linear" for smooth movement.

---

### VERSE (bars 8–24 typically)
**Goal**: Maintain interest without blowing your load. Subtle movement keeps attention without pulling focus from the lyric/melody.

**What to automate**:
- **LFO rate on filter** (subtle): A very slow LFO (0.1–0.5 Hz) on filter cutoff gives pads a breathing quality without being audible as an effect
- **Volume micro-automation**: Ride individual elements up and down by 1–2 dB to keep the mix alive. A pad that's static at -12 dB feels different from one that breathes between -13 and -11 dB.
- **Reverb pre-delay**: Extending pre-delay slightly (from 10ms to 20ms) pushes elements back in space without changing reverb amount
- **Pan automation** (very subtle): Moving a hi-hat or shaker slowly across the stereo field creates unconscious width and interest

**Specific settings**:
- LFO rate: 0.1–0.3 Hz for breathing effect
- Volume rides: ±1–2 dB maximum (invisible but felt)
- Pre-delay range: 8–25ms over 8 bars
- Pan movement: maximum 10–15% left or right over 4 bars

**What NOT to automate in verse**: Filter cutoffs dramatically, reverb wet dramatically, anything that calls attention to itself — the verse is about the content, not the production.

---

### PRE-CHORUS / BUILD (bars 24–32 typically)
**Goal**: Create tension and forward pull. The listener should feel something is about to happen even if they don't know what.

**What to automate — this is where automation gets intentional**:

**Tension builders**:
- **Filter cutoff upward sweep**: In the last 2–4 bars before the chorus/drop, open the filter cutoff from mid-range (2–4 kHz) to fully open. The ear hears this as "something is coming."
- **Reverb wet increasing**: Push reverb from 20% to 60% over the pre-chorus. The track gets more atmospheric, more "uncertain" — creates psychological space for the drop.
- **LFO rate increasing**: If you have an LFO on a filter or effect, automate its rate to increase (from 1/4 note to 1/8 note). The texture gets faster without adding notes.
- **White noise riser**: A separate white noise track with volume automation from silence to -6 dB over 4–8 bars. Not always audible consciously but adds subconscious tension.
- **High-pass filter removing**: If you've been high-passing bass elements, automate the cutoff DOWN in the pre-chorus to start revealing sub-bass weight.
- **Sidechain compression intensity reducing**: In the build, ease off the sidechain pump to let the pads breathe more fully — makes the drop hit harder by contrast.

**Specific settings**:
- Filter sweep rate: 4 bars from 2 kHz to 16 kHz (logarithmic curve recommended)
- Reverb wet: 20% → 60% over 4 bars
- White noise volume: -∞ → -6 dB over 8 bars
- LFO rate: 1/4 note → 1/8 note over 4 bars

**The snare roll technique**:
Automate snare volume from 0% to 100% over the last 2 bars. Start with the snare inaudible, bring it up as the drop approaches. The brain registers "snare getting louder = drop coming."

---

### DROP / CHORUS (bars 32–48 typically)
**Goal**: Maximum energy, maximum clarity. Less automation here than you think — the power comes from contrast with what came before.

**What to automate**:
- **Filter fully open**: The drop should arrive with filters wide open. The "moment of opening" IS the drop.
- **Reverb wet DOWN**: Counter-intuitively, the drop often sounds biggest with LESS reverb (20% or less). Dry signals hit harder.
- **Sidechain compression engaged fully**: The pumping effect is most pronounced here. Sidechain everything to the kick.
- **Volume automation on specific elements**: If a synth lead enters, automate its volume from 0 to -6 dB over the first bar — a soft entrance into a loud context feels intentional.

**What NOT to automate dramatically in the drop**: Almost nothing. The drop should feel stable and maximal. Let the preceding build do the work.

**Specific settings**:
- Filter cutoff: 16–20 kHz (fully open)
- Reverb wet: 15–25% (drier than breakdown)
- Sidechain ratio: 4:1 or higher, fast attack

---

### BREAKDOWN (bars 48–64 typically)
**Goal**: Emotional release, space, vulnerability. The breakdown is often the most automated section.

**What to automate**:
- **Reverb wet dramatically up**: Push to 60–100% wet on pads and leads. Elements should feel distant, dreamlike, cathedral-like.
- **Low-pass filter on drums**: If drums remain in the breakdown, heavily low-pass them (cutoff at 400–800 Hz). Muted, distant drums feel emotional.
- **Volume of bass elements down**: Pull sub-bass down by 6–12 dB. Breakdowns feel open partly because the low end recedes.
- **Delay wet up**: Increase delay feedback and wet amount. Notes should trail off and blur into each other.
- **Panning automation**: Elements can wander more freely in the stereo field — a breakdown can feel more "lost" than a verse.

**Specific settings**:
- Reverb wet: 60–100%
- Drum low-pass cutoff: 400–800 Hz
- Sub-bass volume reduction: -6 to -12 dB
- Delay feedback: 40–60% (longer trails)

---

### BUILD (returning from breakdown to final drop)
**Goal**: Maximum tension, maximum anticipation. Longer and more intense than the pre-chorus build.

**Apply everything from pre-chorus, doubled**:
- Filter sweep over 8 bars instead of 4
- White noise riser over 16 bars
- Snare roll over 4 bars
- Add: **pitch riser** — a separate tone that rises in pitch to the point of discomfort, then cuts to silence one beat before the drop
- Reverb wet pushes to 80% then CUTS completely at drop moment

**The "drop the floor" technique**:
In the last bar before the drop, automate the kick volume to 0 and the bass to 0. Complete silence or near-silence for one beat makes the drop arrival feel like a physical impact.

---

### OUTRO (bars 80–end)
**Goal**: Mirror the intro, release the energy gracefully.

**What to automate**:
- **Low-pass filter closing slowly**: Opposite of the intro opening. Close from fully open to 200–400 Hz over 8–16 bars.
- **Reverb wet increasing**: As elements disappear, increase reverb so the last sounds feel like they're dissolving into space.
- **Volume riding down**: Gradually lower individual tracks rather than a master volume fade — more control, more musical.
- **Elements dropping out**: Automate individual track volumes to silence over time. Leave the most important element (bass, kick, or the main melodic theme) until last.

## Part 3: Specific Effect Automation Techniques

### Filter Automation
**Low-pass filter sweep (build)**: Automate cutoff frequency from 200 Hz to 16 kHz over 4–8 bars.
**High-pass filter removal**: Automate high-pass cutoff from 500 Hz down to 60 Hz to "open up" bass in a build.
**Resonance automation**: Add subtle resonance (Q) increase during the sweep — makes the filter "scream" slightly at the top of the build.

In Ableton:
- Use Auto Filter device
- Right-click the Frequency knob → "Edit Automation"
- Draw the curve in Arrangement View

### Reverb Automation
**Wet/dry automation**: Never automate reverb dry/wet on a track insert — instead use a return track and automate the send level from the source track. This keeps the dry signal consistent.
**Decay time automation**: Slowly increasing decay time (from 1s to 4s) makes a breakdown feel increasingly vast.
**Pre-delay automation**: Increasing pre-delay (10ms → 40ms) pushes elements further back in space without changing reverb amount.

### Sidechain Automation
**Intensity automation**: Some sidechain setups allow automating the threshold — a higher threshold = less pumping = more natural. Automate threshold UP in verses, DOWN in drops for maximum pump.
**Attack automation**: Slower attack = more of the kick transient gets through before ducking = punchier. Automate attack to slow down in drops.

### Volume Automation Best Practices
- Never automate master volume — automate individual track volumes
- Use volume automation for "musical rides" — bringing elements in and out to serve the arrangement
- 1 dB of automation is often enough — you don't need to be dramatic to be effective
- Use logarithmic curves for natural-sounding fades (Ableton: right-click automation → "Log Ramp")

### Delay Automation
**Feedback automation**: Increasing feedback (20% → 60%) during a breakdown makes notes blur into each other — more atmospheric.
**Delay time automation**: Automating delay time from 1/4 note to 1/8 note creates frantic doubling effect for builds.
**Ping-pong toggle**: Automate stereo toggle on delay to activate in breakdowns for wider, more spatial feel.

## Part 4: Automation Shape Reference

The SHAPE of automation matters as much as what you're automating.

| Shape | Name | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| Diagonal line, slow | Linear ramp | Standard filter opens, gentle builds |
| Exponential curve | Log/Exp ramp | Natural-sounding fades, realistic volume rides |
| Sudden jump | Step | Switching effects on/off, sudden drops |
| S-curve | S-curve | Smooth transitions that accelerate then decelerate |
| Sine wave (LFO) | Cyclic | Tremolo, vibrato, filter breathing |
| Sawtooth (LFO) | Sawtooth | Rhythmic filter pulses, gated effects |

In Ableton: Right-click any automation breakpoint for curve options. Hold Alt while clicking between breakpoints to create a curve.

## Part 5: What NOT to Automate (Common Mistakes)

- **Don't automate EQ during mixing** — EQ should be set and left. Automation on EQ is for creative effect only, not correction.
- **Don't automate compression threshold** — Unless you specifically want the dynamic range to shift audibly, set compression and leave it.
- **Don't over-automate the drop** — More automation in the drop = less impact. The drop should feel like arrival, not continuation.
- **Don't automate reverb wet on insert effects** — Always automate the send level on return tracks instead.
- **Don't use automation to fix a bad mix** — Automation is arrangement, not mixing. Fix the mix first.
- **Don't make automation too fast** — Automation that changes faster than the listener can perceive it creates subtle artifacts, not the effect you want.

## Part 6: Automation Quick Reference by Emotion

| You want the track to feel... | Automate... |
|---|---|
| Like it's building toward something | Filter cutoff upward + white noise riser |
| Like it's dissolving | Reverb wet up + low-pass filter closing |
| Like something's about to drop | Snare roll volume up + silence before beat 1 |
| Bigger and wider | Delay ping-pong on + reverb up |
| More intimate and close | Reverb wet down + high-pass on pads |
| More tense and anxious | LFO rate increasing on filter |
| More spacious and dreamlike | Decay time up + pre-delay up + volume down on bass |
| Like it's falling apart | Bitcrusher rate down + filter closing + volume riding |
| Like it's arriving | All filters fully open simultaneously |
| Warmer and more analog | Saturation/tape emulation drive up subtly |
