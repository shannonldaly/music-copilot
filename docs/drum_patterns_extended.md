## Part 1: Underrepresented Electronic Drum Patterns

These patterns go beyond basic four-on-the-floor and standard trap. When users ask for something beyond the obvious genres, these are the patterns to reach for.

### 1. UK Garage / Speed Garage (UKG)
**BPM**: 130–135
**Feel**: Syncopated, swung, rhythmically complex — the kick doesn't always land where you expect

**Signature Pattern** (16 steps at 130 BPM, 1/16 grid):
```
KICK: [x . . . . . x . . x . . . . . .]
SNR:  [. . . . x . . . . . . . x . . .]
HAT:  [x x x x x x x x x x x x x x x x]
PERC: [. . x . . . . x . . . x . . . .]
```
**Key characteristics**:
- Kick on beat 1 and the "and" of beat 2 (anticipation)
- Snare on beat 2 AND beat 3.5 (the "rolling" UKG snare)
- Tight closed hi-hats on every 16th
- Pitched percussion fills (congas, rim shots) on offbeats
- Sub bass plays a melody, not just root notes

**Teaching note**: UKG is built on syncopation. The kick anticipates beats rather than landing on them. This creates a "rolling" feel that's distinct from both 4/4 house and trap half-time.

**Splice search terms**: "UK garage drum loop 130", "2-step loop", "speed garage break"

### 2. Halftime / Neuro-Funk (130–180 BPM played at half-time feel)
**BPM**: 160–175 (feels like 80 BPM)
**Feel**: Massive, lurching, weighted — every hit feels like a physical impact

**Signature Pattern** (16 steps, counts as 8 "half-time" beats):
```
KICK: [x . . . . . . . . . . . x . . .]
SNR:  [. . . . . . . . x . . . . . . .]
HAT:  [. . x . . . x . . . x . . . x .]
SUB:  [x . . x . . . . x . . . . x . .]
```
**Key characteristics**:
- Massive kick on beat 1, nothing until beat 3 (snare)
- Hi-hats sparse and syncopated — not constant
- Sub bass has its own melodic/rhythmic pattern
- Space is intentional — the silence is as important as the hits
- Often processed through heavy distortion and compression

**Teaching note**: Halftime feel is about weight and space. The long gap between kick and snare (beats 1 and 3 at half-time) creates a sense of something massive about to fall. Used extensively in Skrillex, Clozee, and modern bass music drops.

**Splice search terms**: "halftime drum loop", "neuro funk break", "heavy half time groove"

### 3. Broken Beat (Nu-Jazz / Brownswood)
**BPM**: 95–115
**Feel**: Human, imprecise, jazz-influenced — feels like someone almost playing a groove but not quite

**Signature Pattern**:
```
KICK: [x . . . x . . x . . . . x . x .]
SNR:  [. . . x . . . . . x . . . . . .]
HAT:  [x . x x . x . x x . x . x x . x]
RIM:  [. . . . . . x . . . . x . . . .]
```
**Key characteristics**:
- Kick placement is highly syncopated and asymmetric
- Snare doesn't always land on 2 and 4
- Hi-hat patterns change bar-to-bar — rarely repeats exactly
- Rimshot/sidestick adds jazz feel
- Overall groove feels "broken" but still propulsive

**Teaching note**: Broken beat deliberately subverts the grid. Where 4/4 house and trap are highly repetitive, broken beat keeps the ear guessing while maintaining momentum. Artists like Floating Points, Yussef Kamaal, Kaidi Tatham.

**Splice search terms**: "broken beat loop", "nu-jazz drums", "Floating Points style groove"

### 4. Footwork / Juke
**BPM**: 155–165
**Feel**: Frantic, machine-gun, physically intense — designed for footwork dancing

**Signature Pattern**:
```
KICK: [x x . x x . x . x x . x . x x .]
SNR:  [. . x . . x . x . . x . x . . x]
HAT:  [x x x x x x x x x x x x x x x x]
BASS: [x . x . x . x . x . x . x . x .]
```
**Key characteristics**:
- Kick is everywhere — machine-gun patterns, doubles and triples
- Bass drum plays as a melodic instrument, not just a beat anchor
- Everything is at 160 BPM but the rhythmic density makes it feel even faster
- Chicago origin — developed specifically for footwork dancing style

**Teaching note**: Footwork sounds chaotic but has deep internal logic. The kick patterns follow the dancer's feet. Arca, DJ Rashad, Jlin are key artists. This pattern works as a reference for "what if the kick wasn't on the downbeat at all."

**Splice search terms**: "footwork loop 160", "juke drums Chicago", "DJ Rashad style break"

### 5. Jersey Club
**BPM**: 130–140
**Feel**: Bouncy, energetic, vocal chop-driven

**Signature Pattern**:
```
KICK: [x . . x . x . . x . . x . x . .]
SNR:  [. . . . x . . . . . . . x . . .]
HAT:  [x x . x x x . x x x . x x x . x]
```
**Key characteristics**:
- Kick pattern is syncopated with characteristic "3 and" placement
- Hi-hats have a distinctive pattern with 16th triplet feel in sections
- Vocals are chopped rhythmically as percussion elements
- Baltimore Club is the predecessor

**Splice search terms**: "jersey club loop", "club music NJ break", "bouncy club drums 135"

---

### 6. Jungle / Old School Drum & Bass
**BPM**: 150–170
**Feel**: Organic, breakbeat-based, raw — sounds like a break record played at double speed

**Key characteristics**:
- Amen break as foundational sample — B3 snare with specific roll pattern
- Heavily timestretched and pitch-shifted
- Offbeat "ghost" snares and rimshots
- Sub bass on downbeats, Reese bass on the offbeats
- Sampling (not programming) is central to the aesthetic

**Teaching note**: Jungle predates drum & bass and is rawer. The Amen break (originally from "Amen, Brother" by The Winstons) chopped up is the genetic code. When timestretched to jungle tempos, artifacts become part of the character.

**Splice search terms**: "jungle break 170", "amen break chop", "old school DnB loop"

---

### 7. Afrobeat / Afro House
**BPM**: 120–125
**Feel**: Polyrhythmic, organic, communal — multiple rhythmic layers that interlock

**Signature Pattern**:
```
KICK:  [x . . x . . x . . . x . x . . .]
SNR:   [. . . . x . . . . . . . x . . .]
CONGA: [x . x . x . x x . x . x . x . x]
SHAK:  [x x . x x x . x x x . x x x . x]
```
**Key characteristics**:
- Multiple percussion layers interlocking (polyrhythm)
- No single element carries the groove alone
- Organic, human sounds — live congas, shakers, hand percussion
- Kick is syncopated but steady underneath
- Spiritual and communal energy in the rhythm

**Splice search terms**: "afro house drums 120", "afrobeat percussion loop", "tribal house groove"

---

### 8. Polyrhythmic Electronic (3 against 4, 5 against 4)
**Feel**: Hypnotic, shifting, mathematically complex

**3-against-4 example** (at 120 BPM, 12-step loop):
```
3-BEAT: [x . . x . . x . . x . .]
4-BEAT: [x . . x . . x . . x . .]  overlaid
Result: kicks hit at positions 1, 4, 7, 10 while the 4/4 lands at 1, 4, 7, 10 differently
```
**Teaching note**: True polyrhythm means two rhythms with different period lengths running simultaneously. 3-against-4 creates a pattern that doesn't repeat until 12 steps. This is a tool for trance-like states in electronic music — used by Deadmau5, Aphex Twin.

**Splice search terms**: "polyrhythmic drum loop", "triplet overlay percussion", "3 against 4 groove"

## Part 2: Drum Pattern Choice Framework

When a user requests a drum pattern, return ONE primary pattern and THREE alternatives — similar to the chord progression framework.

**Format**:
- **Primary**: Best match for the genre/mood
- **Simpler**: A stripped-down version for production beginners
- **More Complex**: A version with additional syncopation or polyrhythm
- **Left Field**: A surprising option from a different genre that might work

**Example for "dark lo-fi trap at 85 BPM"**:
- Primary: Lo-Fi Basic — kick on 1, snare on 3, swung 16th hi-hats, heavy swing
- Simpler: 4/4 with just kick-snare-hat, minimal extras
- More Complex: Broken beat influence — kick syncopated, snare not always on 3
- Left Field: Jersey Club hybrid at half-speed — bouncy kick pattern with lo-fi hat texture

## Part 4: Teaching Notes on Drum Pattern Psychology

These notes help the Teaching Agent explain WHY certain patterns feel the way they do.

### Why Four-on-the-Floor Works
The kick on every beat (1-2-3-4) provides a predictable, physical pulse that syncs with the human heartbeat range. It's the lowest common denominator of dance music — your body knows what to do with it instinctively.

### Why Half-Time Feels Heavy
When the snare moves from beat 2 and 4 to ONLY beat 3 (in a 4/4 feel), every element gets twice the space to breathe. The longer silence between hits makes each hit feel more weighted and impactful. This is why dubstep drops feel heavier than house drops at the same BPM.

### Why Swing Makes Grooves Feel Human
Swing moves every other 16th note slightly later than the grid. The ear hears this as "lazy" or "laid back" — it matches how a human drummer naturally plays, who also can't be perfectly on the grid. Even 5–10% swing on hi-hats transforms a robotic loop into something that feels alive.

### Why Breakbeats Feel Different From Programmed Drums
Breakbeats are samples of actual drummers. They contain microtime variations (the drummer was slightly ahead here, slightly behind there), velocity variation (no two hits are the same volume), and room sound (the acoustic space of the recording). Programming can approximate this but never quite replicate it — this is why lo-fi and hip-hop productions specifically reach for breakbeats.

### Why Polyrhythm Creates Trance
When two rhythmic patterns with different period lengths run simultaneously (3-beat against 4-beat), the combined pattern takes longer to repeat before it cycles. The brain tracks patterns; when the pattern is too long to hold in working memory, it stops trying and enters a more passive, trance-like state. This is a feature, not a bug.
